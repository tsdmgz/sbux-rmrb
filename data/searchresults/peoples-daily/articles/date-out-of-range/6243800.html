Guangzhou wakes up and smells the coffee
+ -
15:07, August 20, 2007

 Related News

 Print Format  Save Article
A real coffee culture is starting to brew in this city of 10 million inhabitants. The arrival of Starbucks in 2004 kicked off caf culture and offered locals a place to linger, meet friends and relatives, read a book or even study.

Starbucks now has 13 outlets in the city and has paved the way for Italian brands such as illy (aiming to open its first mainland outlet next year) and Lavazza (two outlets). It has also boosted opportunities for local companies, such as Frapesso and SPR coffee, which both have four outlets apiece.

According to the recently formed Coffee Association of Guangzhou (CAGZ), coffee imports to China are increasing 35 to 40 percent a year.

Demand is driven by the young - university students and white collar professionals - attracted to the laid back but hip atmosphere cafs offer, says Dickson Pun, vice-chairman of CAGZ.

"In mature coffee markets like Germany, consumption is around three cups a day per person," says Pun. "In China, average daily consumption is 0.11 cups. So the potential for the market is very big."

To see where all this might lead, we only need to look at Hong Kong, a city of 7 million, where Starbucks has opened 84 outlets since its debut in 2000, local chain Pacific Coffee has 50 outlets and even fast food king McDonalds has entered the field with 38 McCafes.

"Before, coffee drinkers went to Taiwanese-style coffee houses that sold not only coffee but food also," he says.

But better incomes have fuelled a desire for a new lifestyle and a better coffee experience, he adds. Besides, "Chinese are always attracted to foreign things".

In a real coffee culture, coffee drinkers are purists, demanding, appreciative and eager to experience new coffee tastes. The CAGZ is keen to develop the emerging culture by organizing trade shows and training more baristas. It is now in talks with the local government to introduce certification for baristas.

While the slick international brands get most of the credit for the boom, demand for a "pure" coffee drinking experience is rising and this will drive the need for baristas, Pun says.

"At the moment there are around 300 dedicated coffee shops out of 2,000 outlets that offer coffee in Guangzhou," he says. "But in our yearly barista competitions, we only receive around 20 to 30 applicants, so there are not many real baristas working in China yet."

In Guangzhou, "we probably need around 200 baristas," the CAGZ executive believes.

These are places that will take time to fill. For the culture to continue to grow, baristas are needed, but local salaries of 1,000 to 2,000 yuan ($132-$263) a month or 2,000 to 3,000 yuan ($263-$395) at the more upmarket outlets, are not attractive enough to experienced foreigners, who might receive 2000 euros a month in European countries such as Germany or Italy.

"The few foreigners that come here will be from the (mass market) brands, or will set up their own cafs. They will not come as staff," he says.

Currently, foreign baristas are used for short-term training classes only, such as those provided by Lavazza, while training for the bulk of locals is left to more experienced staff who may have had some overseas training.

The vacuum has lead to a lack of depth in the skills taught. But it also represents an opportunity for local baristas to emerge. As coffee culture grows, "people want to learn more about coffee", says Liu Shu, head instructor at Lavazza's training center in Shanghai, who also spends time in Guangzhou. "A real barista understands coffee culture, which coffee is good, how to use the machines, how to make good coffee and how to present coffee to his customers.

"He also has to understand customers' tastes and know which coffee to introduce to them. Most cafs and hotels employ bartenders, coffee makers. There are not many real baristas working here yet."

But the future looks promising.

If the popularity of coffee continues to grow as predicted and promotion and training efforts pay off, the more developed market will likely see the two sides come together as more foreigners arrive to tap the market and impart skills and knowledge to locals.

"Good baristas will be more and more in demand," says Pun.
