Playing Beijing's multifaceted dating game: "Sex-less in the city?"

09:12, November 03, 2009

Beijing can be a tough town for singles seeking love and marriage.

Chinese bachelors and bachelorettes often tell me they don't want to "waste time" playing the dating game. Fei Fei, an attractive 27-year-old, says flatly, "I'm not looking for a friend. I'm looking for a boyfriend who will become my husband!"

In China a date is never just about having a good time together. It's a marriage audition. "It's not just two people getting married, it's two families getting married," says Gigi a gorgeous Chinese girl from Xi'an, working as a film production assistant in Beijing. She tells me about the "matching doors" concept of dating in China. It's the age-old idea that a couple can live in harmony only if their families enjoy the same social status and income level.

"In the past, a couple in love wouldn't get married if they were still living with their parents. Now, even before marriage, they move in with the parents and live together as a couple."

Ancient Chinese poetry may trill on and on about the spiritual joy of lovers gathering chry-santhemums together during the Moon Festival, but the stark reality of relationships here is "show me the money."

My newfound friend Mr Zhang, who runs his own language school and tour business in Beijing, quotes a Chinese proverb translated as, "Money isn't everything, but without it, you are nothing!"

I tell him about a female friend in Shanghai whose boyfriend says he can't marry her until he earns more money than she does. That's a no-win situation as his fiancée is VP of an import-export company. Her yearly earnings are more than a million yuan ($146,400) plus an expense account.

Zhang just shrugs, "That's a Chinese concept that will never change. The man has to be the main provider for the family."

I tell him about an American friend who just married an heiress. Her family is so wealthy that the bride is taking college courses in philanthropy, studying the most effective way to give away her money. All of the groom's male friends say he "married well" and wish we were so lucky.

"Not in Beijing," says Zhang. "Here, the girl's family would say, 'What! You married a poor man!' In Beijing, that marriage would never work out."

Zhang says single guys have a "200 day rule" here. If he meets the girl of his dreams, he should give himself 200 days to see if his infatuation fades, sex loses it ecstasy and nagging little flaws in her personality begin to surface.

That sounds a little cold, but consider Fei Fei. She says that since college, she has had five relationships. Only after we talk do I realize that by "relationship" she means a guy she met once over coffee at Starbucks. Five men, five coffee dates and she rejected all five of them.

"One gentleman had his own apartment and his own car but he asked me, 'If we get married, will you treat me like an employee?' I thought, 'What kind of talk is that?' He called me later and with tickets to the opera. I told him I was busy."

More recently, "relationships" in her life are the lengthy emails she exchanges with three bachelors she contacted on an Internet dating service. She has not met any of them in person.

I mention that in the US, it is widely rumored that half the men on singles dating sites are married men looking for mistresses or one-night stands.

"In China, there are other websites for guys who just want to have sex," she says, noting that many Internet dating subscribers must register with their ID cards and residence permits. On her dating profile, under "income," she put down less than half her yearly salary, aware that Chinese guys won't date her unless they earn more.

Fei Fei says a man's looks aren't important and she would never be swept off her feet by a pretty boy, what the Chinese call a "little milk man."
"I'm prepared to be disappointed by the men I meet online," she adds. "You need a lot of patience and a lot of hope."
